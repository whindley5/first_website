# COMM1180 Value Creation — Week 8: Value for Investors

> **Lecturer:** Dr Natalie Oh, School of Banking & Finance, UNSW Sydney

---

## Overview & Learning Objectives

This week applies prior knowledge of time value of money (Week 5) and investment decisions
(Week 7) to the question of how investors value financial assets — specifically **bonds** and
**shares**. By the end of this week you should be able to:

1. Explain key features of **equity** and **debt** instruments.
2. **Calculate prices** of bonds and stocks.
3. **Value shares and companies** under different scenarios:
   - Constant Dividend (zero-growth) model
   - Constant Dividend Growth (Gordon Growth) model
   - Using Free Cash Flows (DCF) — conceptual understanding
4. Explain the **reinvestment problem** and when a firm should retain vs. pay out earnings.

> ⚠️ Use the **formula sheet** and a **calculator** (not Excel) to solve exam problems.

---

## Motivation: Why Value Companies?

Different stakeholders need valuations for different reasons:

- **Retail investors** — "What is a fair price to buy shares?"
- **Management / analysts** — "At what price should we sell a division?"
- **Private equity firms** — "What should we pay to acquire a company?"

Real-world examples include Bain Capital's acquisition of Virgin Australia and analyst
commentary on Apple stock fair value.

---

## Valuation Techniques: A Taxonomy

### Relative Valuation
Value a company by **comparing it to similar firms** using financial ratios and "multiples"
(e.g., P/E ratio). This is **Comparable Company Analysis**, covered in COMM1140.

### Absolute Valuation
A **bottom-up** approach: forecast individual cash flows into the future and discount them to
find today's **intrinsic value**, accounting for risk. Two key methods:

- **Dividend Discount Models (DDM)** — discount future dividends
- **Discounted Cash Flow models (DCF)** — discount free cash flows to the firm

Both DDM and DCF are the focus of this week.

## Learning Objective 1 — Equity vs. Debt Instruments

Most companies raise capital from **multiple classes of providers**:

| Instrument | Held by | Valuation concept |
|---|---|---|
| Loans | Banks | Market value of debt |
| Commercial notes | Institutional investors | Market value of debt |
| Bonds | Fixed income investors | Market value of debt (net of cash) |
| Shares | Shareholders | Market capitalisation = Price × Shares |

### Perspectives of Firm Value

**Equity Value** represents the shareholders' residual claim on assets and cash flows.

**Enterprise Value (EV)** represents the cost of buying the entire business — it includes all
capital providers.

$$EV_0 = E_0 + D_0 - \text{Cash}_0$$

Where $E_0$ is market capitalisation, $D_0$ is market value of debt, and Cash is subtracted
because an acquirer "inherits" it.

## Learning Objective 2 — Calculating Prices of Bonds and Stocks

### The Fundamental Valuation Principle

> *"The price of any financial asset today equals the sum of all present values of future cash
> flows generated by the asset."*

This principle underlies **all** asset pricing.

- For **shares**: $P_0$ = PV of all future dividends + PV of eventual sale proceeds $P_t$
- For **bonds**: $P_0$ = PV of all coupon payments + PV of face value at maturity

---

### A: Pricing Bonds

A bond's price is the present value of its coupon annuity plus the present value of its face
value (principal), discounted at the **yield to maturity (YTM)**:

$$PV = C \left(\frac{1 - (1+r)^{-n}}{r}\right) + \frac{F}{(1+r)^n}$$

Where:
- $C$ = periodic coupon payment
- $r$ = yield to maturity per period
- $n$ = total number of periods to maturity
- $F$ = face (par) value

> **Key insight:** When YTM > coupon rate, the bond trades at a **discount** (below face
> value). When YTM < coupon rate, it trades at a **premium**.

#### Worked Example — Urbayn's Bond

A bond with face value $\$100{,}000$, coupon rate 6% (semiannual), matures April 28, 2028.
Valued as of April 28, 2026 with YTM = 7.2%.

- Semiannual coupon: $C = \$3{,}000$
- Periods per year: 2 → periods to maturity: $n = 4$
- Rate per period: $r = 7.2\% / 2 = 3.6\%$

$$PV = 3{,}000 \left(\frac{1-(1.036)^{-4}}{0.036}\right) + \frac{100{,}000}{(1.036)^4} \approx \$97{,}800$$

Since YTM (7.2%) > coupon rate (6%), the bond trades **below** face value — consistent with
the discount rule.

---

### B: Pricing Shares

Shares are priced as the present value of all future cash flows to the equity holder. The
**cost of equity** $r_e$ (also called the required rate of return) is the appropriate discount rate.

**Holding for one period:**

$$P_0 = \frac{D_1 + P_1}{1 + r_e}$$

**Holding for two periods:**

$$P_0 = \frac{D_1}{1+r_e} + \frac{D_2 + P_2}{(1+r_e)^2}$$
**Holding for $n$ periods:**
$$PV = C \left(\frac{1 - (1+r)^{-n}}{r}\right) + \frac{F}{(1+r)^n}$$

**Holding indefinitely (general DDM):**

$$P_0 = \frac{D_1}{1+r_e} + \frac{D_2}{(1+r_e)^2} + \frac{D_3}{(1+r_e)^3} + \cdots$$


> Note: determining $r_e$ (cost of equity) is covered in Week 9 using the CAPM / WACC
> framework.

## Learning Objective 3 — Dividend Discount Models (DDM)

DDMs require the company to **pay dividends** that are **predictable and stable**. When
dividends are irregular or absent (e.g., Qantas during COVID), DDMs are limited — motivating
the DCF approach.

---

### Case 1: Constant DDM (Zero Growth Model)

**Assumption:** Dividends are constant forever — $D_1 = D_2 = D_3 = \cdots = D$

This is a **perpetuity**, so:

$$P_0 = \frac{D_1}{r_e}$$

Solving for $r_e$:

$$r_e = \frac{D_1}{P_0}$$

This tells us the **expected return equals the dividend yield** — there are no expected capital
gains, meaning $P_0 = P_1 = P_2 = \cdots$

#### Example — Energex Ltd.

Energex just paid $D_0 = \$2$ and will maintain this forever. Regulators set $r_e = 7.5\%$.

$$P_0 = \frac{\$2}{7.5\%} = \$26.67$$

---

### Case 2: Constant Dividend Growth Model (Gordon Growth Model)

**Assumption:** Dividends grow at a **constant rate $g$ forever**, starting from $D_1$.

Each subsequent dividend: $D_t = D_1 \times (1+g)^{t-1}$

This is a **growing perpetuity**, giving:

$$P_0 = \frac{D_1}{r_e - g} = \frac{D_0(1+g)}{r_e - g}$$

> ⚠️ This formula is **only valid when $r_e > g$**. If $g \geq r_e$, the price would be
> infinite or negative — economically meaningless.

**Return decomposition:**

$$r_e = \underbrace{\frac{D_1}{P_0}}_{\text{Dividend yield}} + \underbrace{\frac{P_1 - P_0}{P_0}}_{\text{Capital gains rate}}$$

In the Gordon Growth Model, the capital gains rate equals $g$ — so the **stock price grows at
the same rate as dividends**.

#### Example — Simon Property Group

Paid $D_0 = \$8$; expected growth $g = 4\%$; cost of capital $r_e = 8\%$.

**Q1: Current price**

$$P_0 = \frac{\$8 \times 1.04}{8\% - 4\%} = \frac{\$8.32}{0.04} = \$208$$

**Q2: Dividend at end of year 3**

$$D_3 = D_0(1+g)^3 = \$8 \times (1.04)^3 = \$9.00$$

**Q3: Share price at end of year 3**

$$P_3 = \frac{D_4}{r_e - g} = \frac{D_3(1+g)}{r_e - g} = \frac{\$9.00 \times 1.04}{0.04} = \$234$$

---

### Case 3: Two-Stage Growth Model

**Key insight:** A company's **optimal payout policy depends on its growth stage**. Fast-growing
firms may pay no dividends early on, then transition to stable payouts.

**Structure:**
- **Stage 1 (near-term):** High/irregular growth — it is possible that $g \gg r_e$ temporarily.
  Dividends are forecast individually for each period.
- **Stage 2 (long-run):** Stable, perpetual growth where $g < r_e$.
  The Gordon Growth Model applies from this point onward.

**Valuation steps:**
1. Discount each Stage 1 cash flow individually to $t=0$.
2. At the transition point $T$, compute a terminal value using the Gordon Growth Model.
3. Discount that terminal value back to $t=0$.
4. Sum all present values.

---

### Case 4: DDM with Deferred Start

When a company temporarily pays **no dividends** and will only begin paying at some future
date $t = T+1$, the valuation follows two steps:

**Step 1:** At time $T$ (one period before the first dividend), the share value is a standard
growing perpetuity:

$$P_T = \frac{D_{T+1}}{r_e - g}$$

**Step 2:** Discount $P_T$ back to today:

$$P_0 = \frac{P_T}{(1+r_e)^T}$$

#### Example — Urbayn (Deferred Dividend)

First dividend of $\$0.15$ per share in **5 years' time**; $g = 5.5\%$ p.a.; $r_e = 12\%$ p.a.

$$P_4 = \frac{D_5}{r_e - g} = \frac{\$0.15}{12\% - 5.5\%} = \frac{\$0.15}{0.065} = \$2.31$$

$$P_0 = \frac{\$2.31}{(1.12)^4} = \$1.47$$

## Learning Objective 4 — The Reinvestment Problem

### Investment and Payout Policy

A firm's earnings each period can be:

- **Paid out** as dividends (Dividend Payout Ratio: $DPO$)
- **Retained** for reinvestment (Retention Rate: $RR$)

$$RR = 1 - DPO$$

### How Dividend Per Share Grows

$$DPS_t = \underbrace{\frac{\text{Total Earnings}}{\text{Shares Outstanding}}}_{EPS_t} \times (1 - RR)$$

To **increase DPS**, a firm can:
1. Grow total earnings
2. Increase the payout ratio ($1 - RR$)
3. Decrease shares outstanding (via **share repurchases**)

---

### The Earnings Growth Formula

When a firm retains and reinvests earnings, future earnings grow:

$$g_t = RR_{t-1} \times ROI_t$$

Where $ROI_t$ is the return on new investment made during period $t$.

#### Example — Urbayn

Earnings = $\$500{,}000$; $RR = 40\%$; $ROI = 20\%$

$$\text{New Investment} = \$500{,}000 \times 40\% = \$200{,}000$$

$$\Delta\text{Total Earnings} = \$200{,}000 \times 20\% = \$40{,}000$$

---

### The Trade-Off in Payout Policy

Substituting $g = RR \times ROI$ into the Gordon Growth Model:

$$P_0 = \frac{D_1}{r_e - g} = \frac{EPS_1(1-RR)}{r_e - g}$$

Retaining earnings **reduces the numerator** (smaller dividend) but **also reduces the
denominator** (higher $g$). The net effect depends on the quality of investment opportunities:

| Scenario | Outcome |
|---|---|
| $ROI > r_e$ | Retaining earnings **increases** $P_0$ — future growth more than compensates |
| $ROI < r_e$ | Retaining earnings **decreases** $P_0$ — foregone dividends not worth the growth |
| $ROI = r_e$ | Payout policy is **irrelevant** — $P_0$ unchanged |

> **Key insight:** Retaining and reinvesting increases share price **only if** $ROI > r_e$.

---

### Worked Comparison — Firm A vs. Firm B

Both firms: $EPS_1 = \$10$, $r_e = 12\%$, $RR = 50\%$

| | Firm A ($ROI = 16\%$) | Firm B ($ROI = 8\%$) | Full Payout ($RR = 0$) |
|---|---|---|---|
| $D_1$ | $\$5$ | $\$5$ | $\$10$ |
| $g$ | $50\% \times 16\% = 8\%$ | $50\% \times 8\% = 4\%$ | $0\%$ |
| $P_0$ | $\frac{5}{0.12-0.08} = \$125$ | $\frac{5}{0.12-0.04} = \$62.50$ | $\frac{10}{0.12} = \$83.33$ |

- **Firm A** ($ROI = 16\% > r_e = 12\%$): retention **creates value** → $P_0 = \$125 > \$83.33$
- **Firm B** ($ROI = 8\% < r_e = 12\%$): retention **destroys value** → $P_0 = \$62.50 < \$83.33$

---

### Share Repurchases

Dividends are **not the only** way to return cash to shareholders. In a **share repurchase**,
the firm uses excess cash to **buy back its own stock**.

Two consequences for DDM:
1. **Less cash available** for dividends in the short run.
2. **Fewer shares outstanding** → $EPS$ and $DPS$ both increase (usually).

In practice (e.g., Apple 2014–2023), **share repurchases can dwarf dividends** — Apple's
dividends accounted for only ~20% of total shareholder payouts. This raises a fundamental
limitation of DDM for such firms.

---

## Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) Model — Introduction

When dividends are absent, irregular, or dominated by repurchases, the **DCF model** offers
a more comprehensive valuation by using **Free Cash Flow to the Firm (FCFF)**.

$$FCFF = EBIT \times (1 - \text{Tax Rate}) + \text{Depreciation} - \Delta NWC - \text{Net CapEx}$$

FCFF represents cash available to **all** capital providers (equity + debt), so it is discounted
at the **WACC** (Weighted Average Cost of Capital) rather than $r_e$:

$$EV_0 = \frac{FCFF_1}{1+WACC} + \frac{FCFF_2}{(1+WACC)^2} + \cdots + \frac{FCFF_t + V_t}{(1+WACC)^t}$$

To find **equity value per share**:

$$E_0 = EV_0 - D_0 + \text{Cash}_0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad P_0 = \frac{E_0}{\text{Shares Outstanding}}$$

> Full numerical treatment of DCF and WACC is deferred to **Week 9**.

## Key Takeaways

1. **The fundamental valuation principle is universal:** Whether pricing a bond or a share, the
   value of any financial asset equals the sum of the present values of its future cash flows.
   The only things that differ across asset types are the nature of the cash flows and the
   appropriate discount rate ($r$ for bonds, $r_e$ for equity, $WACC$ for the whole firm).

2. **Dividend growth only creates value when $ROI > r_e$:** Retaining earnings to fund
   reinvestment is a double-edged sword — it grows future dividends but sacrifices current
   payouts. The net effect on share price is positive **only** when the return on new
   investment exceeds shareholders' required return. This directly links Week 7's investment
   decision framework (positive NPV projects) to investor value.

3. **DDM has real limitations, motivating the DCF model:** For companies that pay no
   dividends or return most cash via repurchases (e.g., Apple), the Dividend Discount Model
   is inadequate. The DCF approach — discounting **free cash flows to the firm** at the WACC
   — provides a more robust and widely used alternative that captures value regardless of
   payout policy.
